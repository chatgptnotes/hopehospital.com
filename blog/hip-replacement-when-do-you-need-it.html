Understanding Hip Replacement Surgery

Hip replacement surgery, also known as total hip arthroplasty (THA), is a surgical procedure where a damaged or diseased hip joint is replaced with an artificial joint (prosthesis) made of metal, ceramic, or plastic components.

The hip joint is a ball-and-socket joint where the rounded head of the femur (thighbone) fits into the cup-shaped socket of the pelvis (acetabulum). When this joint becomes damaged due to arthritis, injury, or disease, the smooth cartilage that cushions the bones wears away, causing bone-on-bone friction that results in severe pain and limited mobility.

Signs You May Need Hip Replacement Surgery

According to Dr. B.K. Murali, the decision for hip replacement surgery is based on the level of pain and disability, not just X-ray findings or age. Here are the key indicators that it might be time to consider hip replacement:

1. Persistent Hip Pain

The most common reason for hip replacement is chronic, severe hip pain that:

  • Persists despite rest, medication, and physical therapy
  • Limits everyday activities like walking, bending, or climbing stairs
  • Disrupts sleep - pain even while lying down
  • Occurs in the groin, thigh, buttocks, or sometimes radiates to the knee
  • Worsens with activity and improves only minimally with rest

2. Limited Range of Motion

  • Difficulty putting on shoes and socks
  • Trouble getting in and out of cars
  • Inability to climb stairs comfortably
  • Stiffness that limits hip movement
  • Difficulty with basic activities like sitting or standing

3. Failure of Conservative Treatments

Hip replacement is typically considered when non-surgical treatments have failed, including:

  • Pain medications (NSAIDs, acetaminophen)
  • Physical therapy and exercise
  • Weight management
  • Walking aids (cane, walker)
  • Corticosteroid injections
  • Activity modifications

4. Reduced Quality of Life

  • Unable to work or perform job duties
  • Social isolation due to limited mobility
  • Loss of independence in daily activities
  • Depression or anxiety related to chronic pain
  • Inability to enjoy hobbies or recreational activities

Don't Wait Too Long!

Many patients delay hip replacement surgery unnecessarily, enduring years of pain and limited mobility. This can lead to:

  • Weakened muscles around the hip, making recovery longer
  • Development of compensatory gait patterns causing knee, back, or other joint problems
  • Decreased overall health due to reduced physical activity
  • More complicated surgery due to severe joint damage

Early consultation with an orthopedic surgeon can help you make an informed decision about the right timing for surgery.

Common Conditions That Lead to Hip Replacement

1. Osteoarthritis

The most common reason for hip replacement (approximately 60-70% of cases). This "wear and tear" arthritis occurs when the protective cartilage cushioning the hip joint gradually breaks down, leading to:

  • Bone-on-bone friction
  • Bone spurs (osteophytes)
  • Progressive pain and stiffness
  • More common in people over 50, but can occur earlier

2. Rheumatoid Arthritis

An autoimmune disease causing inflammation of the joint lining (synovium), which can damage cartilage and bone. Rheumatoid arthritis can affect multiple joints simultaneously and often requires hip replacement at a younger age.

3. Avascular Necrosis (Osteonecrosis)

Death of bone tissue due to lack of blood supply, which can cause the femoral head to collapse. Causes include:

  • Traumatic injury (hip fracture or dislocation)
  • Long-term steroid use
  • Excessive alcohol consumption
  • Certain medical conditions affecting blood flow

4. Hip Fracture

Particularly common in elderly patients with osteoporosis. When the femoral neck fractures, partial or total hip replacement may be the best treatment option, especially if the blood supply to the bone is compromised.

5. Developmental Hip Dysplasia

A condition where the hip socket doesn't fully cover the ball of the femur, leading to premature arthritis. This congenital condition can lead to hip replacement in younger adults.

6. Post-Traumatic Arthritis

Arthritis that develops following a severe hip injury or fracture, even if it occurred years earlier.

Post-Surgery Precautions (First 6-12 Weeks)

To prevent hip dislocation and ensure proper healing, Dr. Murali provides specific precautions:

  • Avoid bending your hip more than 90 degrees
  • Don't cross your legs at knees or ankles
  • Don't twist or pivot on the operated leg
  • Use elevated toilet seat and shower chair
  • Sleep on your back or unoperated side with pillow between legs
  • Avoid low chairs - use chairs with armrests

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What is the ideal age for hip replacement?

There's no strict age limit. The decision is based on pain level and disability, not age. Most patients are between 50-80 years old, but younger patients with severe arthritis or older patients in good health can also benefit.

Q: How long do hip replacements last?

Modern hip implants typically last 15-20 years or longer. Over 90% of hip replacements are still functioning well after 15 years.

Q: Will I be able to walk immediately after surgery?

Yes! At Hope Hospital, patients typically begin walking with a walker on the first day after surgery under physiotherapist guidance.

Q: Is hip replacement painful?

Post-operative pain is managed effectively with medications. Most patients report that recovery discomfort is far less than the chronic pain they experienced before surgery.

Q: Can I have both hips replaced at once?

Bilateral hip replacement (both hips) can be done in selected healthy patients. Dr. Murali will assess if you're a suitable candidate based on your overall health.

Q: What activities can I do after hip replacement?

Low-impact activities like walking, swimming, golf, cycling, and dancing are encouraged. Avoid high-impact activities like running or contact sports.

Q: Will I need to take special precautions forever?

Hip precautions (avoiding extreme bending, crossing legs) are typically needed for 6-12 weeks. After full recovery, most restrictions are lifted, though extreme range of motion should always be avoided.
