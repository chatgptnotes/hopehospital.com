Stroke Emergency — Every Minute Counts (The Golden Hour)

A stroke occurs when blood supply to part of the brain is suddenly cut off — either by a clot (ischaemic stroke) or a burst blood vessel (haemorrhagic stroke). Brain cells begin dying within minutes. The faster treatment begins, the more brain can be saved.

Hope Hospital provides 24/7 stroke emergency care including clot-busting thrombolysis and surgical clot removal (thrombectomy). Time from symptom onset to treatment is everything.

Recognise a Stroke — Use FAST

Act immediately if you notice any of these signs:

  • F — Face drooping: One side of the face droops or feels numb. Ask the person to smile — is it uneven?
  • A — Arm weakness: One arm is weak or numb. Ask them to raise both arms — does one drift downward?
  • S — Speech difficulty: Speech is slurred, strange, or the person cannot speak or understand speech.
  • T — Time to call: If you observe ANY of these signs, call 112 immediately or rush to Hope Hospital's 24/7 Emergency — 071-22980073.

Do not wait for symptoms to improve on their own. Every minute without treatment, approximately 1.9 million brain cells die.

Neurologist vs Neurosurgeon — What Is the Difference?

Patients often ask whether they need a neurologist or a neurosurgeon. Here is the practical difference:

Specialist What They Do Common Conditions Treated
Neurologist Diagnoses and treats brain/nerve disorders with medication and therapy (non-surgical) Epilepsy, migraine, Parkinson's, multiple sclerosis, neuropathy, dementia
Neurosurgeon Performs surgical procedures on the brain, spine, and peripheral nerves Brain tumour, spine disc problems, stroke clot removal, head injury, hydrocephalus, epilepsy surgery

When Should You See a Neurologist?

Many people delay seeking neurological care, attributing symptoms to stress or fatigue. Here are 8 symptoms that warrant prompt evaluation by a neurologist or neurosurgeon:

See a Neurologist Promptly If You Experience

  • Sudden severe headache — described as "the worst headache of my life" (could indicate a ruptured aneurysm)
  • Weakness or numbness on one side of the face, arm, or leg
  • Seizures or blackouts — first episode or change in existing seizure pattern
  • Vision changes — sudden blurring, double vision, or loss of vision in one eye
  • Difficulty speaking or understanding speech
  • Persistent dizziness or loss of balance and coordination
  • Memory loss or sudden confusion that is new or rapidly worsening
  • Neck pain with fever and stiff neck — possible meningitis, a neurological emergency

For any sudden neurological symptom, call 071-22980073 immediately — do not wait for a regular appointment.

Headache: Normal vs Dangerous — Warning Signs

Most headaches are tension-type or migraines and are not dangerous. However, certain headache features demand urgent evaluation:

Feature Likely Benign Seek Emergency Care
Onset Gradual, builds over hours Sudden, thunderclap onset ("worst of life")
Pattern Episodic, similar to previous headaches First-ever severe headache / new type
Associated symptoms Nausea, light sensitivity (migraine) Fever + stiff neck, vision loss, weakness, seizure
Age & context Young adult with known migraine history Headache after head injury, age 50+, cancer history
Response to pain relief Improves with OTC medication and rest Does not improve, progressively worsening

When in doubt, seek evaluation. A thunderclap headache is a neurological emergency until proven otherwise.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the warning signs of a stroke? How do I use FAST?

Use FAST: F — Face drooping (one side droops or is numb, uneven smile), A — Arm weakness (one arm drifts down when raised), S — Speech difficulty (slurred, strange, or absent speech), T — Time to call 112 or rush to Hope Hospital Emergency (071-22980073) immediately. What is the difference between a neurologist and a neurosurgeon?

A neurologist manages brain and nerve conditions medically (epilepsy, migraine, Parkinson's, neuropathy). A neurosurgeon operates on the brain, spine, and nerves (tumour removal, disc surgery, stroke clot removal, epilepsy surgery). What are the risks of brain tumour surgery?

Risks include temporary neurological deficits (weakness, speech changes), infection, bleeding, and anaesthesia complications — depending on tumour size and location.
