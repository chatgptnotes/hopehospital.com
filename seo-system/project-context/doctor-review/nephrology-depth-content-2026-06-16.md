## Section 1 — Understanding the 5 Stages of Chronic Kidney Disease

Chronic Kidney Disease is graded into five stages based on your eGFR (estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate) — a measure of how well your kidneys filter waste. Knowing your stage helps our nephrologists decide whether the goal is to slow progression, prepare for dialysis, or plan a transplant.

- **Stage 1 (eGFR 90+):** Normal kidney function but with signs of damage (e.g. protein in urine). Focus is on controlling diabetes and blood pressure.
- **Stage 2 (eGFR 60–89):** Mild loss of function. Still largely symptom-free; managed with lifestyle and risk-factor control.
- **Stage 3a / 3b (eGFR 45–59 / 30–44):** Moderate loss. This is when most patients are first referred to a nephrologist — anemia, bone, and blood-pressure issues are actively managed.
- **Stage 4 (eGFR 15–29):** Severe loss. We begin preparing for kidney replacement therapy — dialysis access (AV fistula) or transplant evaluation.
- **Stage 5 (eGFR below 15):** Kidney failure (end-stage renal disease). Dialysis or a kidney transplant is needed to sustain health.

Early referral matters: starting nephrology care at Stage 3 rather than Stage 5 can meaningfully slow progression and improve outcomes.

## Section 2 — Hemodialysis vs Peritoneal Dialysis: Which Is Right for You?

There are two main ways to do dialysis. Our nephrology team helps each patient choose based on their kidney function, lifestyle, home environment, and overall health.

- **Hemodialysis (HD):** Blood is filtered through a machine, usually in three sessions a week of about four hours each, at our on-campus NephroPlus unit. Best for patients who prefer trained staff to manage each session.
- **Peritoneal Dialysis (PD / CAPD):** Uses the lining of your own abdomen to filter blood, and can be done at home — including overnight (automated PD). Offers more independence and flexibility for working patients, with training and support from our team.

Both options are covered for eligible cardholders under Ayushman Bharat (PMJAY) and MJPJAY.

## Section 3 — Dialysis or Kidney Transplant: Understanding Your Options

For patients reaching kidney failure, dialysis and transplant are both life-sustaining — but they suit different situations. Dialysis can begin immediately and requires no surgery or donor, while a successful kidney transplant can offer a fuller lifestyle and freedom from regular dialysis sessions, at the cost of major surgery and lifelong immunosuppressant medication.

The right choice depends on your age, overall health, donor availability, and personal preference — a decision our nephrology and transplant teams make together with you and your family.

## Section 4 — Two new FAQ entries

**Q: What is a normal creatinine and eGFR level?**
Creatinine is a waste product filtered by the kidneys; eGFR estimates filtering capacity from your creatinine, age, and sex. A normal eGFR is 90 or above. An eGFR persistently below 60 for three months or more indicates chronic kidney disease, and below 15 indicates kidney failure. Our nephrologists interpret these alongside urine tests and your medical history rather than a single reading.

**Q: Can chronic kidney disease be reversed?**
Most chronic kidney disease cannot be fully reversed, but in its early stages its progression can be significantly slowed — and sometimes stabilised — by controlling diabetes and blood pressure, adjusting medication and diet, and avoiding kidney-harming painkillers. Acute kidney injury, by contrast, is often reversible with prompt treatment. Early nephrology referral gives the best chance of preserving function.
